The VCR Spec — Section by Section

What makes this primitive novel isn't the cryptography. It's the data structure. Here's what each part does and why.

The Core Problem

Diagram 1: Why Agents Break Trust

So when an agent calls your agent and says "I ran this model and got this output," you have no way to verify it. No proof it happened. No way to know who did it. No way to trace where that output came from.

The Solution: Three Things at Once

Diagram 2: What is a VCR?

No blockchain. No token. No consensus protocol. Just a data structure with enough fields to make verified AI outputs tradeable and traceable.

Building the Receipt

Diagram 3: Identity — One Hash Does Two Jobs

Identity Section (2 fields)
receipt_id
SHA-256 of all fields (except signature). Both identifier and integrity check.
schema_version
Protocol version. Currently 1. For future upgrades.

Why this matters: One hash does two jobs. You can look up the receipt by its ID, and you can verify nothing was modified by recomputing the hash.

Diagram 4: Computation — Proof + Model + Data

Computation Section (8 fields)
model_id, verification_key_id
What model was used and where to get its verification key.
input_hash, output_hash
SHA-256 hashes of inputs and outputs. Hashes preserve privacy.
proof, public_inputs, proving_backend
The cryptographic proof itself. Opaque to the protocol—could be ZK, TEE, or future systems.
timestamp
When the computation happened.

Why this matters: Anyone can verify the computation happened and was correct. You don't need to trust the original producer. Just math.

Diagram 5: Provenance — The DAG Structure

Provenance Section (2 fields)
parent_receipts
Ordered list of parent receipts (receipt_id + receipt_hash). Empty for root computations.
provenance_depth
Longest path from this receipt to a root. 0 = root.

Why this matters: You can trace any output back to its origins. The chain is tamper-evident. Modify a parent and all descendants break.

Diagram 6: Economics — Pricing and Royalties

Economics Section (5 fields)
provider
Public key of the entity that performed the computation.
original_price, currency
What was paid for the original computation. Denomination is flexible.
royalty_terms
Provider royalty (basis points), parent royalty (basis points), cascade (recursive).
transfer_count
Times transferred. Mutable state, not part of canonical hash.

Why this matters: You define once, the protocol enforces forever. No negotiation per sale. No platform.

Diagram 7: Integrity — Signature and Proofing

Why this matters: You can cryptographically verify the provider actually signed this receipt. Can't fake it.

How It All Works Together

Diagram 8: Canonical Serialisation

Why this matters: Different languages must produce the same hash. If they don't, the whole system breaks. Canonical serialisation guarantees it.

Diagram 9: Verification — Five Checks

All five pass? The output is real. Verified by math, not trust.

Diagram 10: Settlement — Double-Spend Prevention

Why this matters: No blockchain needed. Certificate Transparency logs (used for HTTPS) do exactly this. Proven, running today.

Why This Primitive is Novel

The VCR is not new cryptography. It's a new data structure that combines proof + provenance + economics in one self-verifying object. Bitcoin solved double-spending without a central authority. The VCR solves verified output without a central authority. Same principles, new domain.

Proof: Agents can verify each other without trusting each other.

Provenance: You can trace any output to its origins. The entire dependency graph is tamper-evident.

Economics: Royalties flow automatically through the provenance chain. Operators earn forever. No platform rent. No negotiation per sale.

Ownership: Transparency logs prevent double-spending. Simple. Proven. Decentralised by default.
